Assuming you're in Hoboken, New Jersey, a 15% rent increase is not automatically legal or illegal. Your rights depend on (1) whether your unit is rent-controlled, (2) whether you're mid-lease or at renewal, and (3) whether the increase is "unconscionable."

In New Jersey, a landlord cannot raise rent in the middle of a lease term. They generally have to terminate the old lease properly and offer a new lease at the higher rent. New Jersey's Anti-Eviction Act also says the tenant has the right to remain if they pay any legal increase, and the landlord must give proper written notice before trying to evict over a rent increase.

Also in New Jersey, a rent increase cannot be "unconscionable". The state's tenant guide says courts look at things like: the size of the increase, the landlord's expenses/profitability, how the new rent compares with similar units nearby, and the parties' bargaining positions. A 15% increase is large enough that it is at least worth checking carefully, especially if it jumps well above comparable rents.

For Hoboken specifically, this matters a lot: if your apartment is subject to Hoboken rent control, the city says annual increases are generally capped at the lower of 5% or CPI. Hoboken also has a Rent Leveling & Stabilization Office, an appeal process, and an Illegal Rent Increase Form.

So the practical answer is:

- If you are mid-lease: they usually cannot just impose +15% now.
- If you are at renewal and the unit is rent-controlled in Hoboken: a 15% renewal increase is very likely a problem unless there is some specific lawful basis. Hoboken's normal cap is much lower.
- If you are at renewal and the unit is not rent-controlled: the landlord may raise rent, but it still cannot be unconscionable and they must give proper notice.

What I'd do next:

1. Check whether your building/unit is covered by Hoboken rent control. The city says tenants can contact the Rent Leveling & Stabilization Office and request a legal rent calculation or file an appeal.
2. Keep the written rent increase notice, your current lease, and the proposed renewal.
3. Compare your proposed new rent with similar units in your building and nearby in case you need to argue the increase is unconscionable. The NJ guide specifically treats comparable rents as relevant.
4. Contact Hoboken's Rent Leveling & Stabilization Office at [rentcontrol@hobokennj.gov](mailto:rentcontrol@hobokennj.gov) or (201) 420-2000 x1721 / x1723. Hoboken also says the city offers free legal counsel on housing matters through its Tenant Advocate.

#### Scenario 2: Your Apartment is NOT Rent-Stabilized (Market-Rate)

**A 15% increase is still likely challengeable** thanks to New York's **Good Cause Eviction law** (effective April 2024). Key points:

- Rent increases above **CPI + 5%** (or **10%**, whichever is lower) are **presumptively unreasonable**
- The current threshold is approximately **~8.8%** — well below your landlord's proposed 15%
- If your landlord tries to evict you for not paying the higher rent, you can use this as a defense in Housing Court
- The landlord would need to prove the increase is justified by specific cost increases (taxes, insurance, maintenance)

**Who's covered:** Market-rate apartments built before 2009, with some exceptions (units renting above ~$6,004/month for a 1-bedroom, or buildings with 10 or fewer units statewide).

| Tenancy length    | Required notice |
